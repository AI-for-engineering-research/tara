Lesson 0001 · ~15 min

The real question is not whether to remove aromatics

Mission tie-in: this lesson is built for research meetings. The point is to make your framing sharper than “lower aromatics = better.”

Win for this lesson

By the end, you should be able to answer this meeting question cleanly:

“What exactly are we optimizing when we lower aromatics in kerosene, and what prevents us from just driving them to zero?”

Core idea

Aromatics removal is not the objective. It is a lever. The objective is to alter the fuel-composition features that most strongly increase soot-particle formation, because those particles can affect contrail-relevant ice nucleation. The lever is limited by fit-for-purpose jet-fuel behavior and by process/system penalties.

That sounds obvious, but in meetings people often slide between four different claims:

  1. lower aromatics
  2. lower soot
  3. lower contrail impact
  4. better climate outcome

Those are not interchangeable claims. Your edge is to keep them separated.

The advanced framing move

Instead of asking:

How much aromatic removal can we achieve?

ask:

Which composition changes buy the largest reduction in soot-relevant behavior per unit penalty in fuel properties, hydrogen, yield, and cost?

This reframes the work from a purity target to a constrained optimization problem.

Pressure-test yourself

Write short answers, then reveal the benchmark below.

Prompt A

A collaborator says: “If aromatics drive soot, then the ideal jet fuel is just zero-aromatic kerosene.” What is your 2–3 sentence response?

Prompt B

In one sentence, distinguish goal, mechanism, and constraint.

Benchmark answers

Prompt A: Zero aromatics is not automatically optimal because the decision variable is not aromatic content alone but climate-relevant particulate behavior under jet-fuel constraints. Deep aromatic removal can create penalties in materials compatibility and key fuel properties, and the process itself may require more hydrogen, severity, cost, or carbon burden than is justified by the incremental emissions benefit.

Prompt B: The goal is to reduce soot-relevant emissions and thereby potentially reduce contrail climate impact; the mechanism is composition-dependent particle formation behavior; the constraint is that the fuel must still satisfy jet-fuel performance and be producible at acceptable process and system cost.

Where strong researchers sound stronger

  • They say “soot-relevant aromatic fraction” or “composition features” when total aromatics is too blunt.
  • They say “can reduce contrail-relevant ice nuclei availability” instead of promising contrail elimination.
  • They explicitly name the counterweights: seal swell / elastomer issues, density, process severity, H2 demand, yield, and cost.
  • They make clear that the best target may be selective reduction, blending, or reformulation, not absolute minimization.

Mini drill: upgrade weak statements

  1. “Aromatics cause contrails.”
  2. “Removing aromatics helps the climate.”
  3. “Lower-aromatic kerosene is better fuel.”

Rewrite each one to be technically safer and more precise. If you want, send me your versions and I’ll critique them line by line.

Takeaway

The mature framing is: low-aromatic kerosene is a constrained emissions-control strategy, not a purity project.
