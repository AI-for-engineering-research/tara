Updates

I tried using the teach me skill and had it teach me my research project: the purpose, benefits, and tradeoffs of low-aromatic jet fuel. The conversation is here: teach me skill conversation, and it produced this lesson: The real question is not whether to remove aromatics.

I thought the lesson did a good job clarifying the reality of low-aromatic jet fuel, especially the constraints and downsides. It clearly captured that the correct answer is not simply “lower aromatics means better fuel and lower climate impact.” There are many tradeoffs that need to be assessed.

I am already very knowledgeable on this topic, so there was not much that this quick lesson could teach me. But I was impressed with its understanding of the topic, and I decided to use this skill next on something I knew much less about: how LLMs work.

In a second session, I used the teach skill to set up an LLM learning workspace and work through the basics of transformers interactively. The conversation is here: LLM teach skill conversation. That session created a small learning structure and helped me understand next-token prediction, the difference between training and inference, why tokens matter, embeddings, positional information, and attention as a relevance mechanism.

By the end, I had a clearer mental model of what an LLM is actually doing at each step, and the next planned lesson was self-attention in more detail: queries, keys, and values. I also saved a handoff summary here: LLM learning workspace handoff. This felt like a much better use case for the skill because I was learning a topic where I genuinely needed the scaffolding.
